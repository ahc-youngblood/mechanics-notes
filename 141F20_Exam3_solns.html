1 A ladder of length \(L\) and mass \(M\) is resting against a frictionless wall. The floor has a coefficient of static friction \(\mu_s\).

1.1 For the ladder alone, find an expression for the minimum angle \(\theta_c\) for which it will not slip. This will depend on one or more (but not necessarily all) of: \(M,\mu_s,g,L\).

We will take the point of contact with the ground to identify the axis of rotation for our system. With this assumption we set up the torque equation:

\begin{align} -Mg\frac{L}{2}\sin(90^\circ - \theta_c) + F_{Nw}L sin\theta_c &= 0\\ -Mg\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta_c + F_{Nw}L\sin\theta_c &= 0 \end{align}

The negative sign is present because if the wall is to the right of the point on the ground where the ladder is supported, the weight of the ladder will tend to make it rotate clockwise (if you drew your diagram a different way all that really matters is that the weight and the normal force of the wall produce opposite torques). We see that we need to find the normal force due to the wall, which will be given by the horizontal force equation:

\begin{align} F_{fr,f} - F_{Nw} &= 0\\ F_{fr,f} &= F_{Nw} \end{align}

Which shows us that we will need to find the friction force due to the floor. We are looking for the minimum \(\theta_c\) so we can use \(F_{fr,f} = \mu_sF_{Nf}\). Therefore, we need \(F_{Nf}\) from the vertical force balance:

\begin{align} F_{Nf} - Mg &= 0\\ F_{Nf} &= Mg\\ \end{align}

Now we can go back through our previous results with this replacement to get

\begin{align} \Rightarrow F_{fr,f} &= \mu_sMg\\ \Rightarrow F_{Nw} &= \mu_sMg \end{align}

And finally

\begin{align} -Mg\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta_c + \mu_sMgL\sin\theta_c &= 0\\ -Mg\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta_c &= -\mu_sMgL\sin\theta_c\\ \frac{1}{2}\cos\theta_c &= \mu_s\sin\theta_c\\ \frac{1}{2\mu_s} &= \tan\theta_c\\ \theta_c &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2\mu_s}\right) \end{align}

1.2 A person of mass \(m_p\) stands \(L/4\) of the ladder (we can treat them as a point mass). If the ladder is at the minimum angle found in part 1.1, give an expression for the torque about the point where the ladder is in contact with the ground due to the person's weight in terms of (some of): \(m_p,\mu_s,L\). [If you have not found \(\theta_c\) in terms of other quantities, you can use it directly]

The presence of the person on the ladder makes two contributions to the torque. First is the direct torque due to their weight, but their presence also changes \(F_{Nw}\). Taking both of these into account

\begin{align} -m_pg\frac{L}{4}\cos\theta - Mg\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta + \mu_s(Mg+m_pg)L\sin\theta &= I\alpha\\ -\left(\frac{m_p}{4} - \frac{M}{2}\right)Lg\cos\theta + \mu_s(M+m_p)Lg\sin\theta &= I\alpha\\ \end{align}

Now, based on the previous part we know that

\begin{align} -Mg\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta_c + \mu_sMgL\sin\theta_c &= 0\\ \end{align}

So at \(\theta=\theta_c\) all that is left is

\begin{align} -\frac{m_p}{4}Lg\cos\theta_c + \mu_sm_pLg\sin\theta_c &= I\alpha\\ m_pLg\left(\mu_s\sin\theta_c - \frac{1}{4}\cos\theta_c\right) &= I\alpha \end{align}

1.3 If the wall is suddenly removed find an expression for the initial acceleration of the ladder with the person on it as described in 1.2.

Now, the moment of inertia of the system is the combined moment of inertia of the ladder about the point of contact with the floor and the person treated as a point mass with respect to the same point of contact. So \(I= m_p(L/4)^2 + (1/3)ML^2\). So we have

\begin{align} \alpha &= \frac{m_pLg\left(\mu_s\sin\theta_c - \frac{1}{4}\cos\theta_c\right)}{m_p(L/4)^2 + (1/3)ML^2} \end{align}

However, the first term in the numerator is eliminated because the wall is absent so:

\begin{align} \alpha &= \frac{m_pLg\left(- \frac{1}{4}\cos\theta_c\right)}{m_p(L/4)^2 + (1/3)ML^2} \end{align}

2 A solid spherical ball of mass \(m\) and radius \(R\) rolls (starting from the top) down an incline of length \(l\) and angle \(\theta\).

2.1 In terms of quantities given and \(g\), what maximum speed will the sphere reach by the bottom of the incline?

It is best to solve this using energy conservation. We can express the change in height as \(l\sin\theta\) then

\begin{align} mgl\sin\theta &= \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm,max}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega_{max}^2\\ mgl\sin\theta &= \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm,max}^2 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{2}{5}mR^2\omega_{max}^2\\ gl\sin\theta &= \frac{7}{10}v_{cm,max}^2 \end{align}

The last step follows because we know that the ball rolled without slipping. The linear center of mass velocity at the bottom of the incline is then given by

\begin{align*} v_{cm,max} &= \sqrt{\frac{10}{7}lg\sin\theta} \end{align*}

2.2 What is the ball's acceleration down the incline?

Now that we know the final center of mass velocity, we can use linear kinematics (considerations of the rotation have already been included).

\begin{align*} 2a_{cm}l &= v_{cm,max}^2\\ 2a_{cm}l &= \frac{10}{7}lg\sin\theta\\ a_{cm} &= \frac{5}{7}g\sin\theta \end{align*}

Notice that this is less than the acceleration would be if the object had not been rolling.

2.3 What minimum friction force (in terms of \(m\),\(g\),\(\theta\), and numerical constants) is required to keep the sphere rolling without slipping?

In order to not slip, the friction force must be sufficient to reduce the linear acceleration that would have occurred if the object had been sliding to the one calculated above for a sphere that does not slip. We know from previous work that the acceleration without rolling would be \(g\sin\theta\) so

\begin{align} F_{fr,min} &= m\left(g\sin\theta - \frac{5}{7}g\sin\theta\right)\\ &= m\frac{2}{7}g\sin\theta \end{align}

3 A thin, uniform stick of mass \(M\) and length \(L\) is at rest on a flat, frictionless surface to which one end of it is pinned. A small mass \(m\) traveling at speed \(v\) collides with and attaches to the stick at a distance \(2L/3\) away from the end through which it is pinned to the surface.

3.1 Find an expression for the moment of inertia of the stick + mass object after the collision.

We should first identify the axis about which we want to determine the moment of inertia. A good choice is an axis parallel to the pinned end of the stick. The moment of inertia of the stick about this axis is \(\frac{1}{3}ML^2\). The moment of inertia of the small mass about the same axis is \(m(2L/3)^2\). Then the total moment of inertia is

\begin{align*} I_{tot }= \frac{1}{3}ML^2 + \frac{4}{9}mL^2 \end{align*}

3.2 Find an expression for the final angular speed of the combined object.

The initial angular momentum of the system was that of the small mass so

\begin{align} I_m\omega_{m} &= I_{tot}\omega_f\\ m\left(\frac{2L}{3}\right)^2\frac{v}{(2L/3)} &= \left(\frac{1}{3}ML^2 + \frac{4}{9}mL^2\right)\omega_f\\ \frac{mv\frac{2L}{3}}{\left(\frac{1}{3}ML^2 + \frac{4}{9}mL^2\right)} &= \omega_f\\ \frac{2mv}{\left(ML + \frac{4}{3}mL\right)} &= \omega_f \end{align}

4.1 Neptune has been observed to have an orbital period of 165 (Earth) years. By what factor is Neptune's orbital radius greater than Earth's?

From Kepler's third law we know that

\begin{align*} \frac{T_N^2}{T_E^2} &= \frac{R_N^3}{R_E^3}\\ 165^2 = (R_N/R_E)^3\\ ^3\sqrt{27225}R_E &= R_N\\ 30.1R_E &\approx R_N \end{align*}

4.2 One of Neptune's moons, Triton, orbits the planet at an average orbital radius of 354,800 km with an orbital period of 141 hours. What is Neptune's mass?

We equate the centripetal force needed to keep this moon in orbit to the gravitational force acting on it.

\begin{align} m\frac{v^2}{r} &= \frac{GM_Nm}{r^2}\\ \frac{(2\pi r/T)^2}{r} &= \frac{GM_N}{r^2}\\ \frac{4\pi^2}{T^2}r^3 &= GM_N\\ \frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GT^2} &= M_N\\ \frac{(4\pi^2(3.548\times10^8\mathrm{m})^3}{\left(6.674\times10^{-11} \frac{\mathrm{N}\cdot \mathrm{m}^2}{\mathrm{kg}^2}\right)(5.076\times10^5\mathrm{s})^2} &= M_N\\ 1.025\times 10^{26} \mathrm{kg} &\approx M_N \end{align}

5 A sealed, rectangular box of dimensions 9 cm \(\times\) 12 cm \(\times\) 5 cm is floating in water with its 9 cm \(\times\) 12 cm face parallel to the bottom of the water container. The top 1.5 cm of the sealed box are out of the water. (You may ignore the density of air for this problem).

5.1 What is the effective density of the box (if it was solid, the material would have a density of …)?

The box must have the same mass as the displaced volume of water so

\begin{align} \rho_{eff}V_{obj} &= \rho_{H_2O}V\\_{sub}\\ \rho_{e}ff &= \rho_{H_2O}\left(\frac{V_{s}ub}{V_{obj}}\right)\\ \rho_{eff} &= \rho_{H_2O}\left(\frac{Ah_{sub}}{Ah_{obj}}\right)\\ \rho_{eff} &= \rho_{H_2O}\left(\frac{h_{sub}}{h_{obj}}\right)\\ \rho_{eff} &= (1 \frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{m}^3})\left(\frac{3.5\mathrm{cm}}{5\mathrm{cm}}\right)\\ &= 0.7 \frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{m}^3} \end{align}

5.2 You are told that the box is made of Aluminum, which has a density of 2.7 g/cm\(^3\). Assuming that the box is empty, what fraction of the total volume of the box is air?

We ignore the density of air so we assume it has a density of 0 g/\(m^3\).

\begin{align} \rho_{eff} &= \frac{\rho_{Al}V_{Al} + \rho_{air}V_{air}}{V_{tot}}\\ \rho_{eff} &= \rho_{Al}\frac{V_{Al}}{V_{tot}}\\ \frac{\rho_{eff}}{\rho_{Al}} &= \frac{V_{Al}}{V_{tot}} \end{align}

It is also true that \(V_{air} + V_{Al} = V_{tot}\) so

\begin{align} \frac{V_{air}}{V_{tot}} &= 1-\frac{V_{Al}}{V_{tot}}\\ &= 1-\frac{\rho_{eff}}{\rho_Al}\\ &= 1-\frac{0.7 \text{g/cm}^3}{2.7 \text{g/cm}^3}\\ &\approx 0.74 \end{align}

So about 74 percent of the sealed box is air.
