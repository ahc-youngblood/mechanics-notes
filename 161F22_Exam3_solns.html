1.1 Neptune has been observed to have an orbital period of 165 (Earth) years. By what factor is Neptune's orbital radius greater than Earth's?

From Kepler's third law we know that

\begin{align*} \frac{T_N^2}{T_E^2} &= \frac{R_N^3}{R_E^3}\\ 165^2 = (R_N/R_E)^3\\ ^3\sqrt{27225}R_E &= R_N\\ 30.1R_E &\approx R_N \end{align*}

1.2 One of Neptune's moons, Triton, orbits the planet at an average orbital radius of 354,800 km with an orbital velocity of 4.4 km/s. What is Neptune's mass?

We equate the centripetal force needed to keep this moon in orbit to the gravitational force acting on it.

\begin{align} m\frac{v^2}{r} &= \frac{GM_Nm}{r^2}\\ \frac{v^2r}{G} &= M_N\\ \frac{(4.4\times 10^3 \mathrm{m/s})^2(3.548\times 10^8\mathrm{m})}{\left(6.674\times 10^{-11} \frac{\mathrm{N}\cdot \mathrm{m}^2}{\mathrm{kg}^2}\right)} &= M_N\\ 1.025\times 10^{26} \mathrm{kg} &\approx M_N \end{align}

2 A rod of length \(L\) with square-root linear mass density \(\lambda(x) = c_0x^{1/2}\) is resting on a flat, frictionless surface and is pinned to that surface through its center of mass.

2.1 In terms of \(L\), find an expression for the distance of the center of mass of the stick from the nearest end.

To find the location of the center of mass we will need the total mass in terms of \(L\) and \(c_0\) so we must integrate the given \(\lambda(x)\)

\begin{align*} M=\int_0^L c_0x^{1/2} dx &= 2c_0\frac{L^{3/2}}{3}\\ \end{align*}

Now we can find

\begin{align*} x_{cm} &= \frac{1}{M}\int_0^L xc_0x^{1/2} dx\\ &= \frac{1}{M}2c_0\frac{L^{5/2}}{5}\\ &= \frac{3}{5}L \end{align*}

The problem asks for the distance from the nearest end, which is \(\frac{2L}{5}\).

2.2 A small mass \(m\) traveling at speed \(v\) collides with and attaches to the stick at the end furthest from the center of mass. Find

2.2.1 The final moment of inertia of the stick + mass.

We will need the moment of inertia of the nonuniform stick so we begin with that. Ultimately we will need the moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass, but it is easier to compute it about one end (we will choose the less dense end) and then apply the parallel axis theorem.

\begin{align*} I_{end} &= \int_0^L x^2c_0x^{1/2} dx\\ &= c_02\frac{L^{7/2}}{7}\\ \end{align*}

Now apply the parallel axis theorem

\begin{align*} I_{end} &= I_{CM} + Md^2\\ I_{CM} &= I_{end} - Md^2\\ &= c_02\frac{L^{7/2}}{7} - c_02\frac{L^{3/2}}{3}\left(\frac{3L}{5}\right)^2\\ &= c_02\frac{L^{7/2}}{7} - c_0\frac{18L^{7/2}}{75}\\ &= c_0\frac{L^{7/2}(150 - 126)}{525}\\ &= \frac{12}{175}ML^2 \end{align*}

The moment of inertia of the small mass about the center of mass of the stick is \(\frac{9}{25}mL^2\), so the total moment of inertia is

\begin{align*} I_{tot} &= \frac{12}{175}ML^2 + \frac{9}{25}mL^2 \end{align*}

2.2.2 The final angular speed of the stick + mass.

\begin{align*} I_i\omega_i = m\frac{3L}{5}v &= I_{tot}\omega_f\\ \frac{mv}{\frac{4}{35}ML + \frac{3}{5}mL} &= \omega_f \end{align*}

3 Consider a container filled to a height of 50 cm with water.

3.1 What is the gauge pressure (not counting atmospheric pressure) 15 cm below the surface of the water?

The Gauge pressure is \(p = \rho g h\) with $h = 0.15 m and \(\rho\) for water is 1 g/cm\(^3\) = 1000 kg/m\(^3\) so the gauge pressure is 1470 Pa.

3.2 A small hole opens in the side of the container at this 15 cm depth, what is the speed of the water as it leaves the hole?

Here we use Bernoulli's equation, realizing that the hole is 0.15 meters below the top of the fluid:

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 &= \rho g \delta h\\ v &= \sqrt{2g\delta h} &= 1.71 \mathrm{m/s} \end{align*}

3.3 At what distance from the base of the container will the water land?

The water will be launched horizontally from the container so \(v_iy\) =0 m/s. Find the time it takes the water to fall 0.35 m to the ground:

\begin{align*} -0.35 \mathrm{m} &= -\frac{1}{2}(9.8 \mathrm{m/s^2})t^2\\ 0.27 \mathrm{s} &= t \end{align*}

The water will travel horizontally for this amount of time until it reaches the ground so

\begin{align*} v_xt &\approx (1.71 \mathrm{m/s})(0.27 \mathrm{s})\\ &\approx 0.46 \mathrm{m} \end{align*}

3.4 Now 20 cm of oil with a density of 0.84 g/cm\(^3\) is added at the top, at what new distance from the base of the container will the water land now?

This will add a pressure term \(\rho_{oil}g\delta y_{oil}\) so

\begin{align*} \rho_{oil}g\delta y_{oil} + \rho g \delta y &= \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2\\ \sqrt{2g(\frac{\rho_{oil}}{\rho}\delta y_{oil} + \delta y)} &= v \end{align*}

So the new speed is approximately 2.5 m/s and the distance to the new landing point is 0.67 m from the base of the container.

4 An object is suspended from a string which is found to have a tension of 20 N in this situation.

4.1 What is the mass of the object?

20 N/(9.8 m/s\(^2\)) = 2.04 kg

4.2 While still attached to the string, the object is fully submerged in a tank of fluid (Hg) with density 13.5 g/cm\(^3\). If the tension in the string is now 6N, find the density and volume of the object.[Recommendation: figure things out symbolically first.]

\begin{align*} F_T + F_B &= mg\\ F_T + \rho_{f} V_{sub} g &= mg\\ V_{sub} &= \frac{mg - F_T}{\rho_fg}\\ &=\frac{14 \mathrm{N}}{(13500 \mathrm{kg/m^3})(9.8 \mathrm{m/s^2})}\\ &\approx 0.0000106 \mathrm{m^3} \end{align*}

Then \(\rho_{obj} = m/V_{sub}\) or 19.3 g/cm\(^3\).

5 Consider a ladder with a non-uniform linear mass density given by \(\lambda(x) = \beta x\). This ladder rests on ground with a coefficient of static friction \(\mu_s\) and leans against a frictionless wall.

5.1 If the ladder is placed so that its center of mass is as close as possible to the floor, find an expression for the minimum angle \(\theta_c\) that the ladder can make with the floor without slipping.

As we have solved previously, a stick of length \(L\) with a non-uniform density that depends linearly on distance from one end has its center of mass at a distance \(\frac{2}{3}L\) from the least dense end. Then for static equilibrium the force equations will be

\begin{align*} x: F_w - \mu_sF_f &= 0 \mathrm{N}\\ y: F_f - Mg &= 0\mathrm{N}\\ \end{align*}

so

\begin{align*} F_f &= Mg\\ F_w &= \mu_sMg \end{align*}

Now the torque equation, taking the pivot to be the point of contact with the ground:

\begin{align*} LF_w\sin\theta - \frac{1}{3}LMg\sin(90^\circ-\theta) &= 0\mathrm{N\cdot m}\\ L\mu_sMg\sin\theta &= \frac{1}{3}LMg\cos\theta\\ \tan\theta_c &= \frac{1}{3\mu_s}\\ \theta_c &= \tan^{-1} \frac{1}{3\mu_s} \end{align*}

5.2 If the ladder is instead placed so that its center of mass is as far as possible from the floor, find and expression for the minimum angle \(\theta_c\).

Now the only difference is that the force \(Mg\) acts at \(\frac{2}{3}L\) up the ladder from the pivot point on the ground so $tanθ_c = \frac{2}{3\mu_s}

5.3 Is the angle in (b) greater or less than the angle in (a)?

The angle in (b) is greater, that is there is a narrower range of angles for which the ladder is stable when its center of mass is far from the ground.

6 A uniform, circular disk of radius 5 cm and mass 0.5 kg rolls on a flat surface with coefficient of static friction \(\mu_s = 0.3\).

6.1 What is the maximum linear acceleration that the disk can have if is to roll without slipping?

The condition for rolling without slipping is that \(\alpha = a_{cm}/R\). If we are looking for a maximum acceleration then \(F_{fric} = \mu_sMg\). Note, there must also be some other force producting this \(a_{cm}\) but it won't produce any torque about the center of mass of the disk so we ignore it. From Newton's Second Law for rotation (about the center of mass of the disk:

\begin{align*} RF_{fric} &= I_{cm}\alpha\\ R^2F_{fric} &= I_{cm}a_{cm}\\ a_{cm} &= \frac{R^2F_{fric}}{I_{cm}} \end{align*}

The moment of inertia for a uniform disk about its center of mass is \(\frac{1}{2}MR^2\), with the given values this is 5.9 m/s^2.

6.2 If the disk starts at rest and has this acceleration for 4 seconds, find its linear and angular velocities. Also find its linear and angular momentum.

\(a_{cm}t \approx\) 23.6 m/s which corresponds to 472 rad/s for this disk. The linear momentum is 11.8 kg$⋅$m/s and the angular momentum is \(\approx\) 0.3 kg$⋅\(m\)^2$/s
