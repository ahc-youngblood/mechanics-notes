1 The figure on the right (on the exam) represents a free body diagram corresponding to three massless cords under tension pulling on a small mass. Assume that any gravitational force is negligible. Assume that the system is in equilibrium.

1.1 With respect to our standard coordinate system (\(\hat{i}\) positive to the right and \(\hat{j}\) positive upward), find the scalar components \(T_{Ax},T_{Ay},T_{Bx},T_{By},T_{Cx},T_{Cy}\) in terms of the magnitudes \(T_A,T_B,T_C\) and the angle \(\theta_C\).

\begin{align*} T_{Ax} &= -T_A\\ T_{Ay} &= 0\\ T_{Bx} &= 0\\ T_{By} &= -T_B\\ T_{Cx} &= T_C\cos\theta_C\\ T_{Cy} &= T_C\sin\theta_C \end{align*}

1.2 Find the Cartesian (\(\hat{i}\) and \(\hat{j}\) with respect to the same coordinate system) components, magnitude, and angle \(\theta_C\) of the vector \(\vec{T}_C\) in terms of the magnitudes of \(\vec{T}_A\) and \(\vec{T}_B\).

\begin{align*} \vec{T}_C &= T_A\hat{i} + T_B\hat{j}\\ T_C &= \sqrt{T_A^2 + T_B^2}\\ \theta_C &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{T_B}{T_A}\right) \end{align*}

1.3 Find numerical values for part 1.2 if \(T_A\) = 8N and \(T_B\) = 17N.

\begin{align*} \vec{T}_C &= 8\mathrm{N}{i} + 17\mathrm{N}\hat{j}\\ T_C &= \sqrt{(8\mathrm{N})^2 + (17\mathrm{N})^2} = 66N \\ \theta_C &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{17\mathrm{N}}{8\mathrm{N}}\right) = 65^\circ \end{align*}

2 Think of the starter of an outboard motor as a cord wrapped around a solid cylinder (\(I_{cyl} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2\)) of radius \(R_c\). It takes time \(\Delta t\) for the cylinder to reach and angular velocity \(\omega\) when the cord is pulled on by a constant force of magnitude \(F\).

2.1 Find an expression for the mass \(m_c\) of the cylinder in terms of \(R_c\), \(\Delta t\), \(\omega\), and \(F\).

\begin{align*} R_cF &= I\alpha\\ \frac{R_cF}{I} &= \alpha\\ \frac{2F}{m_cR_c} &= \alpha\\ \end{align*}

Also, \(\omega = \alpha\Delta t \Rightarrow \alpha = \frac{\omega}{\Delta t}\). So

\begin{align*} \frac{2F}{m_cR_c} &= frac{\omega}{\Delta t}\\ \frac{2F\Delta t}{\omega R_c} &= m_c \end{align*}

2.2 Find a numerical value for \(m_c\) if $R_c = 7.00$cm, \(\Delta t\) = 2.00 s, \(\omega\) = 500.0 rad/s, and $F = 50.0$N.

$m_c ≈ 2.86$kg

3 A ball is thrown at an angle \(\theta\) up to the top of a cliff of height \(l\) from a point a distance \(l\) from the base of the cliff. Find the initial speed \(v_i\) (in terms of \(\theta\)) for the ball to just hit the edge of the cliff.

From horizontal and vertical displacement with \(t\) being the time taken to reach the edge of the cliff:

\begin{align*} l &= (v_i\cos\theta)t\\ l &= (v_i\sin\theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\\ l &= l\tan\theta - \frac{gl^2}{2v_i^2\cos^2\theta}\\ v_i &= \sqrt{\frac{gL}{2\cos\theta(\sin\theta - \cos\theta)}} \end{align*}

4 An inclined plane of mass \(M\) and angle \(\theta\) is sitting on the floor of an elevator. A block of mass \(m\) is released from rest (relative to the incline) from the top of the incline (which is a height \(h\) above the floor of the elevator).

4.1 If the incline is frictionless and the elevator is accelerating downward with an acceleration of magnitude \(a_d\), find an expression for the time it will take the block to reach the bottom of the incline.

If there were no incline, the magnitude of the acceleration down the incline would be \(g-a_d\), since the incline is present the acceleration parallel to the incline is \(a_{||}=-(g-a_d)\) where we treat up the incline as the positive direction. The displacement, with the same sign convention will be \(\Delta x_{||} = -\frac{h}{\sin\theta}\) so

\begin{align*} -\frac{h}{\sin\theta} &= -\frac{1}{2}(g-a_d)\sin\theta t^2\\ t &= \sqrt{\frac{2h}{(g-a_d)\sin^2\theta}} \end{align*}

4.2 If the coefficient of static friction between the incline and the block in \(\mu_s\), find an expression for the magnitude \(a_u\) of the minimum upward acceleration required to keep the block from sliding down the incline.

Following the same logic as the previous part, the force equation parallel to the incline (for the maximum possible static friction) is:

\begin{align*} -m(g+a_u)\sin\theta + \mu_sm(g+a_u)\cos\theta = 0\\ \tan\theta &= \mu_s \end{align*}

Note that the value of \(a_u\) does not matter, just as the mass and the value of \(g\) (as long as they are not 0) do not matter.

4.3 Find expressions for

4.3.1 The force of the floor of the elevator on the incline in case 1.1.

\begin{align*} (M+m)(g-a_d) \end{align*}

4.3.2 The force of the floor of the elevator on the incline in case 1.2.

\begin{align*} (M+m)(g+a_d) \end{align*}

5 The figure shows a mass \(m_1\) on a plane incline at an angle \(\theta\) connected by a massless, unstretchable cord over a massless, frictionless pulley to a second mass \(m_2\). The coefficient of static friction is \(\mu_s\).

5.1 Draw and label a free body diagram for \(m_1\) in the absence of friction.

5.2 For \(\mu_s = 0, \mu_k \neq 0\), find an expression for the acceleration of \(m_1\)

\begin{align*} m_1a &= T-m_1g\sin\theta - \mu_km_1g\cos\theta\\ m_2a &= m_2g - T\\ a &= g\frac{m_2 - m_1\sin\theta -\mu_km_1\cos\theta}{m_1+m_2} \end{align*}

5.3 For \(\mu_s \neq 0\), find an expression for the minimum \(m_2\) that will keep \(m_1\) from sliding down the incline.

\begin{align*} 0 &= m_2g + \mu_sm_1g\cos\theta - m_1g\sin\theta\\ m_2 &= m_1(\sin\theta - \mu_s\cos\theta)\\ \end{align*}

5.4 For \(\mu_s \neq 0\), find an expression for the minimum \(m_2\) that will cause \(m_1\) to slide up the incline.

In this case the sign of the friction force is reversed so \(m_2 = m_1(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)\).

5.5 If $μ_s = 0.5, μ_k = 0.25, θ = 30^ˆ, m_1 = 200$g and \(m_2\) is the minimum mass from part (d), find the numerical value of the tension in the cord.

\begin{align*} T &= m_1g(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta) \end{align*}
