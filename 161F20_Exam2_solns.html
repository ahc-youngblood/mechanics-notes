2.1 If the bob had speed \(v\) at the lowest point of its swing, what maximum height above this lowest point (in terms of \(m,g,v,l,\theta\) [note: you won't necessarily need all of these for your answer]) will the bob reach?

We use conservation of energy between kinetic and gravitational potential energy to find the maximum height that the bob will reach.

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}mv^2 &= mgh_{max}\\ h_{max} &= \frac{v^2}{2g} \end{align*}

2.2 Find an expression for the angle \(\theta\) that this height corresponds to.

You can use a bit of trigonometry to find that

\begin{align*} h_{max} &= l(1-\cos\theta)\\ \cos\theta &= 1-\frac{h_{max}}{l}\\ \theta &= \cos^{-1}\left(1-\frac{h_{max}}{l}\right) \end{align*}

2.3 Find an expression for the bob's speed at half of this maximum height.

There are a couple of ways of approaching this, we will keep things in terms of the maximum height \(h_{max}\). Since the total available energy is \(mgh_{max}\)

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}mv_{1/2}^2 + mg\frac{h_{max}}{2} &= mgh_{max}\\ \frac{1}{2}v_{1/2} &= mg\frac{h_{max}}{2}\\ v_{1/2} &= \sqrt{gh} \end{align*}

You could for instance compare this to the result of the first part and state that \(v_{1/2} = v/\sqrt{2}\).

3 Two identical springs of spring consant \(k\) are on opposite sides of a flat table as shown in the figure. A block of mass \(m\) compresses the left-hand spring from its equilibrium extension by a distance \(x_c\). The block is released and detaches from the left spring at its equilibrium extension and eventually runs into and compresses the right-hand spring a distance \(d\) away. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the block and the and the surface of the table is \(\mu_k\)

3.1 If \(\mu_k = 0\), find an expression for the speed of the block when it detaches from the first spring in terms of \(g,k,x_c,m,d\).

In the absence of friction we set what was initially the spring's potential energy equal to the kinetic energy of the block so

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}kx_c^2 &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2\\ x_c\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} &= v \end{align*}

3.2 If \(\mu_k = 0\), find an expression for the distance that the second spring is compressed in terms of \(g,k,x_c,m,d\).

In the absence of friction, the second (identical) spring will be compressed the same distance that the first spring was compressed: \(x_2=x_c\).

3.3 If \(\mu_k \neq 0\), find an expression for the speed of the block just as it reaches the second spring and before it is compressed in terms of \(g,k,x_c,m,d,\mu_k\).

Now we have to consider the energy lost as the mass travels the distance \(x_c + d\). Note that the normal force acting on the block is \(F_N = mg\).

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}kx_c^2 - \mu_kmg(x_c+d) &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2\\ \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}x_c^2 - 2\mu_kg(x_c+d)} &= v \end{align*}

3.4 If \(\mu_k \neq 0\), find an expression for the distance that the second spring is compressed in terms of \(g,k,x_c,m,d,\mu_k\).

Frequently, people forget that the friction contribution depends on the distance \(x_2\) that the second spring is compressed so

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}kx_c^2 - \mu_kmg(x_c+d) - \mu_kmgx_2 &= \frac{1}{2}kx_2^2\\ x_c^2 - 2\mu_k\frac{m}{k}g(x_c+d) - 2\mu_k\frac{m}{k}gx_2 &= x_2^2\\ x_2^2 + 2\mu_k\frac{m}{k}gx_2 - (x_c^2 - 2\mu_k\frac{m}{k}g(x_c+d)) &= 0\\ \frac{k}{2\mu_kmg}x_2^2 + x_2 - \left(\frac{k}{2\mu_kmg}x_c^2 - (x_c+d)\right) &= 0\\ x_2 &= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4\left(\frac{k}{2\mu_kmg}\right)\left(\frac{k}{2\mu_kmg}x_c^2 - (x_c+d)\right)}}{\frac{k}{\mu_kmg}}\\ &= -\frac{\mu_kmg}{k} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\mu_kmg}{k}\right)^2 - 4\left(x_c^2 - \frac{\mu_kmg}{k}(x_c+d)\right)} \end{align*}

where we have had to solve a full quadratic equation.

4 A block of mass \(m\) is launched along a frictionless surface by a spring of spring constant \(k\) that was initially compressed a distance \(d\). This block collides elastically with a pendulum bob of mass \(5m\) which is suspended by a cord of length \(l\). (For all parts, assume that the block and the bob have only collided once).

4.1 In terms of \(k\) and \(d\), what is the maximum height to which the bob rises?

Applying energy conservation before the collision

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}mv_{mi}^2 &= \frac{1}{2}kd^2\\ v_{mi} &= \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}d\\ \end{align*}

From the equations on the front page, the velocity of the bob after the collision is

\begin{align*} v_b &= \frac{2m}{m + m_b}v_{mi}\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}d \end{align*}

Now apply energy to the bob conservation after the collision \being{align*} \frac{1}{2}mv_b^2 &= mgh_{max}
\frac{1}{18}kd^2 &= mgh_{max}
h_{max} &= \frac{kd^2}{18mg} \end{align*}

4.2 In terms of \(k\), \(d\), and \(l\) what is the maximum value that the angle \(\theta\) takes on?

\begin{align*} h_{max} &= l(1-\cos\theta_{max})\\ \frac{kd^2}{18mg} &= l(1-\cos\theta_{max})\\ \cos\theta_{max} &= 1-\frac{kd^2}{18mgl} \end{align*}

4.3 What are the maximum and minimum values of the tension in the cord and at what angles \(\theta\) do they occur?

The maximum tension occurs at \(\theta = 0^\circ\) and is equal to \(mg\). The minimum tension occurs at \(\theta_{max}\) and is equal to \(mg\cos\theta_{max}\).

4.4 In terms of \(d\), by what distance is the spring compressed when the block returns?

Again using the equations on the front page:

\begin{align*} \frac{m - m_b}{m+m_b}v_{mi} &= -\frac{2}{3}v_{mi}\\ \end{align*}

Having \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the speed, the block has \(4/9\) of its initial energy or

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}kd_{new}^2 &= \frac{4}{9}\frac{1}{2}kd^2\\ d_{new} &= \frac{2}{3}d \end{align*}

5 An object is traveling to the left with speed \(v\) and a second, identical object incoming from the left at 30\(^\circ\) below horizontal and also with speed \(v\) collides with it elastically. As a result of this collision the first object's final velocity is entirely in the vertical direction but still has magnitude \(v\).

5.1 What is the velocity (magnitude in terms of \(v\) and direction) of the center of mass of the system consisting of the two objects before and after the collision?

Before the collision, the center of mass velocity is

\begin{align*} \vec{v}_{cm} &= \frac{mv\cos\theta - mv}{2m}\hat{i} + \frac{mv\sin\theta}{2m}\hat{j}\\ \vec{v}_{cm} &= \frac{1}{2}(v\cos\theta-v)\hat{i} + \frac{1}{2}v\sin\theta\hat{j}\\ \vec{v}_{cm} &= -.067v\hat{i} + 0.25v\hat{j} \end{align*}

The center of mass velocity is not changed by the collision because no external forces acted so \(v_{cm}\) is remains the same after the collision.

5.2 What is the second object's velocity (magnitude in terms of \(v\) and direction)?

We can combine momentum conservation with the result above to compute the second object's velocity. For the $x$-component:

\begin{align*} mv(\cos\theta - 1) &= mv_{2xf} \end{align*}

For the $y$-component:

\begin{align*} mv\sin\theta &= mv_{1yf} + mv_{2yf} \end{align*}

Energy conservation gives us

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}2mv_{cm}^2 &= \frac{1}{2}mv_{1yf}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m(v_{2xf}^2 + v_{2yf}^2)\\ 2v_{cm}^2 &= v_{1yf}^2 + ((\cos\theta-1)^2v^2 + v_{2yf}^2)\\ v_{1yf}^2 &= 2v_{cm}^2 - ((\cos\theta-1)^2v^2 + v_{2yf}^2)\\ \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} v\sin\theta &= \sqrt{2v_{cm}^2 - ((\cos\theta-1)^2v^2 + v_{2yf}^2)} + v_{2yf}\\ (v\sin\theta - v_{2yf})^2 &= 2v_{cm}^2 - ((\cos\theta-1)^2v^2 + v_{2yf}^2)\\ v^2\sin^2\theta - 2v_{2yf}v\sin\theta + v_{2yf}^2 &= 2v_{cm}^2 - (\cos\theta-1)^2v^2 - v_{2yf}^2\\ 4v_{2yf}^2 - (2v\sin\theta)v_{2yf} + v^2(\sin^2\theta + (\cos\theta-1)^2) - 2v_{cm}^2 &= 0\\ v_{2yf}^2 - \frac{v}{4}v_{2yf} + v_{cm}^2 - \frac{1}{2}v_{cm}^2 &= 0\\ v_{2yf}^2 - \frac{v}{4}v_{2yf} - \frac{1}{2}v_{cm}^2 &= 0\\ v_{2yf} &= \frac{v}{4} \pm \sqrt{\frac{v^2}{16} + 2(.067)v^2}\\ v_{2yf} &= -0.19v \end{align*}

So all together

\begin{align*} v_{2yf} &= -0.14v\hat{i} -0.19v\hat{j} \end{align*}

Where we chose the negative solution because the second object must travel opposite to the first to conserve momentum.
