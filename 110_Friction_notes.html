Notes on Friction

1 Not like other forces: Behaves the most like Normal force.

Like the normal because it only shows up as a reaction. Unlike normal force it has a maximum value: \[ F_{fr,max} = \mu F_N \] Force also needs a direction. \(\vec{F}_{fr}\) points in the direction that opposes motion.

Hint: Figure out motion without friction first, then include friction.

Also, there are two "kinds" of friction, one for objects that are already moving (using \(\mu_k\) for the maximum – but it's always at maximum if it's moving). Another is for objects that are stationary (not yet moving), which uses \(\mu_s\) for the maximum magnitude.

What is the magnitude of static friction if it is not at its maximum value? It will always be the same as is necessary to oppose the sum of the other forces in the system. If only one force \(F\) is present

\begin{align} F < F_{fr,max,static} &\Rightarrow F_{fr} = F; \quad a=0\\ F > \mu_sF_N &\Rightarrow F_{fr} = \mu_kF_N; \quad a = \frac{F-\mu_kF_N}{m} \end{align}

Friction is a surface force, have to consider the component of the force parallel to the surface. Normal force can only act at surface and only perpendicular to it. Friction can only act at a surface and only parallel to it.

Pushing or pulling on a flat surface but with the force at an angle \(\theta\) to the surface, then we only care about \(F\cos\theta\) component (for friction directly). However, we still need the vertical component. Say that I want to find the maximum force I can apply before the object starts "slipping"

\begin{align} F_{max}\cos\theta &= \mu_s F_N\\ &= \mu_s (mg \pm F_{max}\sin\theta)\\ F_{max}\cos\theta \mp F_{max}\sin\theta &=\mu_smg\\ F_{max} &= \frac{\mu_s mg}{cos\theta \mp \sin\theta} \end{align}

Similar application of trignometry comes up if we want to figure out the angle at which an object will start slipping in the absence of any applied forces.

\begin{align} F_N &= mg\cos\theta_{max}\\ F_{fr} &= \mu_smg\cos\theta_{max}\\ \mu_smg\cos\theta_{max} &= mg\sin\theta_{max}\\ \mu_s &= \tan\theta_{max}\\ \theta_{max} &= \tan^{-1}\mu_s \end{align}

Now, what if an additional force \(F\) is applied to the object on the incline? What with the maximum angle be then?

\begin{align} F_N &= mg\cos\theta_{max} \pm F\sin\phi\\ F_{fr} &= \mu_s(mg\cos\theta_{max}\pm F\sin\phi)\\ \mu_s(mg\cos\theta_{max}\pm F\sin\phi) &= mg\sin\theta_{max} - F\cos\phi\\ F(\cos\phi \pm \sin\phi) &= mg(\sin\theta_{max} - \mu_s\cos\theta_{max}) \end{align}

2 Mass \(m_1\) pulled by another mass \(m_2\) freely hanging over a pulley.

\begin{align} m_2g-F_T &= m_2a\\ F_T - F_{fr} &= m_1a \end{align}

To find maximum possible \(m_2\) before the system begins to move:

\begin{align} F_{fr} &= \mu_sF_N = \mu_sm_1g\\ \end{align} \begin{align} m_2g-F_T &= 0\\ F_T - \mu_sm_1g &= 0\\ m_2g - \mu_sm_1g &=0\\ m_2 &= \mu_sm_1 \end{align}

To find the acceleration once the system begins to move:

\begin{align} m_2g-F_T &= m_2a\\ F_T - \mu_km_1g &= m_1a\\ m_2g - \mu_km_1g &= (m_1+m_2)a\\ \frac{m_2 - \mu_km_1}{m_1+m_2}g &= a \end{align}
