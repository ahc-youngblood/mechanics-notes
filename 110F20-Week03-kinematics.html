Kinematics Notes (1D)

1 1D Vector Quantities

\[ \vec{\Delta x} \qquad \mathrm{displacement} \qquad \Delta x \] \[ \frac{\vec{\Delta x}}{\Delta t} = \bar{\vec{v}} \qquad \mathrm{velocity} \qquad \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \] \[ \frac{\Delta(\frac{\vec{\Delta x}}{\Delta t})}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \vec{a} \qquad \mathrm{acceleration} \qquad a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

In 1D even though we have vectors all are along \(\hat{i}\). \(\Delta q\) represents \(q_f - q_i\) or \(q(t) - q(0)\) if \(\Delta t = t - 0\)

1.1 Displacement \(\vec{\Delta x}\): Distance (in a direction) traveled by an object.

Distance, speed, and time. Original way people usually see it is \(d = vt\) or distance = rate \(\times\) time. However, consider a round trip with the farthest point a distance \(l\) away. The total distance traveled is 2\(l\) but the net or total displacement is \(l\hat{i} - l\hat{i} =\vec{0}\). A good analogy is the displacement is like separation on a map while distance is what would be read by a car's odometer for a trip.

1.2 Velocity \(\vec{v}\): Rate of change of displacement with respect to time.

\(\bar{v} = v\) if \(v\) is constant. Because then \[\frac{v(t_1) + v(t_2)}{2} = v\] no matter what \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) are. Can we have a constant velocity \(v\) and still have a round trip? Answer: No, you would have to switch the sign of \(v\).

Now, if \(v\) is constant, then we can say \[ \Delta x = vt \qquad \mathrm{or} \qquad {\Delta \vec{x}} = \vec{v}t \]

This was our first kinematic equation. Kinematics is about finding the relations among displacement, time, velocity, and acceleration. So that if I tell you when and where something started and its initial velocity and acceleration and a time when I want to know where it will be: you can answer.

Example: I was at position 0m and I arrived at a position 7m to the right after 4 seconds. What was my velocity? Answer: \(\frac{\Delta x}{t} = \frac{7m}{4s}(\hat{i}) = \frac{7}{4}\frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}}(\hat{i})\)

1.3 Acceleration \(\vec{a}\): Rate of change of displacement with respect to time.

\[ \mathrm{1D:}\qquad \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v(t) - v(0)}{t} = \frac{v(t) - v_i}{t} = \frac{v(t) - v_0}{t} \]

We will always have \(a\) constant so \(a = \bar{a}\). Now, \[ v(t) = v(0) + at \] This is our second kinematic equation. Example: If we started with a velocity \(v(0)=\) +15m/s and we come to a stop (\(v=\) 0m/s) in 3 seconds, what was our acceleration?

Answer: \(a = \frac{v(t) - v(0)}{t} = \frac{0\mathrm{m/s}-15\mathrm{m/s}}{3\mathrm{s}} = -5 \mathrm{m/s}^2\)

\[ \mathrm{Also:} \qquad v^2 - v_i^2 = 2a\Delta x \]

1.4 Back to displacement. This time with changing \(v\), but constant \(a\)

Put equation for \(v\) as a function of \(t\) into equation for \(v\) as a function of \(\Delta x\) and solve for \(\Delta x\):

\[ \Delta x = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

\[ x_f = x_i + v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

This is our last kinematic equation

1.5 Free Fall: Motion influenced only by gravitational acceleration.

\(g\) = 9.81 m/s\(^2\)

Acceleration due to gravity is \(g (-\hat{j})\). Notice that \(g\) itself is not negative!
